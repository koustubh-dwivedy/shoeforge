# Parametric Shoe Last Design Parameters — PRD Reference for a Goodyear-Welted Men's Dress Shoe Last Generator

## TL;DR
- A men's Goodyear-welted dress-shoe last can be fully parametrized with roughly 35–45 named parameters grouped into eight families: overall dimensions, girths, widths, heights/pitch, bottom/plantar geometry, cross-sectional "wall quadrants," reference landmarks, and surface-quality controls. The single most authoritative parametric scheme in the five primary sources is 3DShoemaker's build model, which reduces the whole last to a small set of "basic/primary measurements" — 3DShoemaker states verbatim, "The basic shoe last measurements are the primary measurements required to design a shoe last. All other measurements are calculated from these" — namely last length, ball girth, waist girth, instep girth, long-heel girth, ball width, and heel height, plus size/width indices, allowances, and a template.
- Last measurements are derived from **foot measurements plus explicit allowances**: length/toe space (~17 mm for a round toe per 3DShoemaker; 12–15 mm everyday to 15–20 mm pointed per Koleff), a feather-edge girth allowance ("The feather edge allowances apply only to the girths and account for the additional girth amount due to the fact that shoe lasts have sharp edges whereas feet don't"), an instep-girth "fill-up" for arch height, sock thickness, and insert/insole depth. Grading between UK full sizes is ~8.47 mm in length (one barleycorn = 1/3 inch; per Wikipedia's *Barleycorn (unit)*: "equal to 1⁄3 of an inch (i.e. about 8.47 mm)… still used as the basis of shoe sizes") and ~6.35 mm in ball girth (1/4 inch), with each width fitting (E→F→G) adding ~6.35 mm of girth.
- For a code-driven Blender/Python pipeline the defensible architecture is: (1) anchor a small set of landmarks (heel point, ball/joint points, instep point, toe point, cone/back-cone top) on a size-scaled skeleton; (2) place girth cross-section curves at those landmarks with quadrant-based control (medial-upper, lateral-upper, medial-lower, lateral-lower); (3) loft NURBS surfaces (sole, upper, top/cone) with tangency/curvature continuity; and (4) apply toe spring, heel height/pitch, and feather-edge geometry as bottom-profile transforms.

## Key Findings
- **The parametric method is cross-section (quadrant) based.** The 3DShoemaker sources and the academic CAD literature agree: a last is represented as girth cross-section curves anchored to landmarks, each curve split into quadrants, then lofted into NURBS surfaces (sole, upper, top). Smoothness is achieved by editing the "body wall curve" and "toe wall curve" and by adjusting the "caps" (highest points) of heel/arch cross-sections relative to the girth cross-sections.
- **Primary vs derived parameters.** The primary set (last length in functional and full form, ball girth, waist girth, instep girth, long-heel girth, ball width, heel height) plus size/width indices, allowances, and a template fully determine every other curve.
- **Landmarks anchor everything.** Ball (1st and 5th metatarsophalangeal joints), instep point (middle-cuneiform prominence), heel point, toe point, and the cone/back-cone are the anatomical anchors; girth planes are placed relative to them. The heel-to-ball (arch) length sits at roughly 63–69% of foot length (US patent 7,770,306: medial ball ≈ 69%, lateral ball ≈ 63% of foot length; Koleff places the joint/tread line at 2/3 of foot length).
- **Allowances convert foot → last** and are documented with numbers (length/toe space, feather-edge girth, instep-girth fill-up, sock thickness 0.5–3 mm by type, insert depth 0.5–3 mm — 3DShoemaker: "The standard insert depth for all shoe lasts is 0.5mm, which is enough space for a sock lining").
- **Goodyear-welt-specific geometry:** the last must have a hard ~90° feather edge and a flat last bottom (seat) so the lasting allowance can be pulled around the featherline and the insole/holdfast can seat flat; this drives the feather-edge allowance and the choice of a "hard" (sharp) rather than rounded feather edge.
- **Numeric anchors (men's, UK):** length ~8.47 mm/full size; ball girth ~6.35 mm/full size and ~6.35 mm per width fitting; typical dress-last heel height ~18–25 mm and toe spring ~13–19 mm (Spring Line product specs). A real Northampton welted maker's last (Joseph Cheaney & Sons "125" last) gives UK 8 F-fit internal length 290 mm / joint girth 245 mm; UK 9 F = 298 mm / 251 mm — confirming +8 mm length and +6 mm ball girth per full size — and F→G = exactly +6 mm joint girth at every size 6–12.5.

### B. Landmarks / reference points (anchor everything else)
- **Heel point (back point):** rearmost central point of the heel; origin for length and heel-girth measurement. *(Podohub/3DShoemaker foot-tracing guide; shoemaking.wiki.)*
- **Ball / joint points (1st & 5th MPJ):** medial (1st metatarsophalangeal) and lateral (5th MPJ) prominences; the ball-girth plane passes through both; the widest part of the last. Medial ball ≈ 69%, lateral ball ≈ 63% of foot length. *(Podohub; US patent 7,770,306.)*
- **Ball line & ball-line point:** the line joining the 1st and 5th ball points; the ball-line point sits at ~41% of the ball-line length from the 1st joint (≈ base of 2nd toe). *(3DShoemaker foot-tracing guide.)*
- **Instep point:** the middle-cuneiform prominence on the dorsal foot; anchors the instep-girth plane. *(3DShoemaker/Podohub.)*
- **Toe point / toe extremity:** forwardmost point of the last including any stylistic toe extension.
- **Cone / back cone:** the raised rear-upper mass of the last above the instep (the "throat/cone" region); the top of the back cone is a key height landmark, and the smoothing article notes the cone is sensitive to cap-height changes.
- **Featherline (feather edge):** per shoemaking.wiki, "a line around the bottom of a last marking the point where the upper will end and the insole will begin" — the corner where the upper surface meets the last bottom. Divides the surface into upper vs bottom.
- **Waist:** the region between ball and instep (narrowest part of the mid-last).
- **Seat / heel seat:** the rounded rear part of the last bottom on which the heel/foot heel rests.
- **Vamp point / throat:** the take-off point on the cone where the topline begins to curve up; sets the practical facing/throat length (mainly a pattern-making landmark, but it constrains cone geometry). *(shoemaking.wiki "Vamp Point.")*

### C. Overall dimensional parameters
- **Last length (full / stick length):** length along the profile curve of the last bottom surface, including any stylistic toe extension; measured "along the curved bottom profile" (mm). Primary size driver.
- **Functional last length:** last length excluding stylistic toe extension — the length that maps to the foot. 3DShoemaker's "Adapt" option treats the specified length as functional and adds the template's stylistic extension.
- **Stick length (traditional):** length measured flat along the ground plane with the heel elevated to the design heel pitch (alternate definition in *Footwear Pattern Making & Last Design*, via shoemaking.wiki).
- **Arch length (heel-to-ball):** distance from heel point to ball-line point along the central axis; ~63–69% of foot length; must align with the shoe's flex line.
- **Toe (stylistic) extension / recede:** the part of the last projecting beyond the toes that forms the toe shape; adds length for pointed/chisel toes.

### D. Girth / circumference parameters (define volume)
All girths are loops (circumferences, mm) on planes anchored to landmarks; they are the core volume controls and each is a primary parameter in 3DShoemaker.
- **Ball girth (joint girth):** circumference through the 1st and 5th ball points (shortest path). The defining "width fitting" measure. Grades ~6.35 mm/full size and ~6.35 mm per width letter. *(3DShoemaker; Shoemakers Academy: "The ball girth of a last typically grades at a rate of 1/4 inch and 6.35mm per whole size.")*
- **Waist girth:** measured on a plane averaged halfway between the ball- and instep-girth planes; typically a few mm smaller than ball girth (Koleff: ~3–5 mm smaller than joint girth).
- **Instep girth:** loop through the instep point (middle cuneiform) around the plantar surface (shortest path); guarantees the foot can enter the shoe.
- **Long heel girth:** the diagonal loop from the instep/cone region around the back of the heel; long heel = short heel + ~40–46 mm (Koleff).
- **Short heel girth:** a shorter diagonal heel loop; used with long heel to define the heel/seat.
- **Ankle girth (for boots / higher lasts):** horizontal loop at medial-malleolus height (requires an accompanying malleolus-height parameter).
- **Ball width ↔ ball girth aspect ratio:** ball width is a separate primary parameter; 3DShoemaker's "Adapt" keeps the ratio of ball width to ball girth constant (or hits an exact width for bespoke), coupling the width and girth controls.

### E. Width parameters (linear, across the last)
- **Ball width (tread width):** straight linear distance across the ball (1st–5th MPJ); the bottom width just behind the ball that contacts the ground.
- **Waist width:** linear width across the waist.
- **Heel (seat) width:** linear width across the heel seat; the foot-tracing method sets a heel-width point at 1/6 of foot length along the heel axis.
- **Toe / tread width:** width across the tread region under the ball.
- **Backpart width / heel-seat width:** rear last-bottom width parameters named in American last-measurement sets (shoemaking.wiki: Backpart Width, Heel Seat Width).

### F. Height / pitch parameters
- **Heel height (heel elevation):** vertical height of the last bottom at the seat above the ground plane; typical men's dress last ~18–25 mm (≈ ¾–1 inch). Primary parameter; "Adapt" grades the template's heel height to the specified length. *(Spring Line product specs: Formal Round Toe 18 mm; Classic Toe Soft Square 22 mm; Fashion Formal Point 25 mm.)*
- **Heel pitch:** the angle/slant of the heel seat under the last (distinct from heel height); "height of shoe last under the foot seat" and the slope that sets stance.
- **Toe spring:** the height of the toe tip above the ground plane (the front rocker); typical dress lasts ~13–19 mm (Spring Line); depends on sole thickness/rigidity and heel height. The traditional balance rule is "pencil under the toe, penny under the heel."
- **Wedge angle:** in 3DShoemaker calculated automatically from heel height unless specified; the effective bottom rocker/heel-to-toe wedge.
- **Toe box height:** vertical clearance over the toes at the toe region; a named last measurement (shoemaking.wiki).
- **Instep height / cone height (back-cone height, front-cone height):** heights of the cone that set instep volume; the smoothing article flags cap heights of the heel/arch cross-sections vs the girth cross-sections as smoothness-critical.
- **Instep-girth fill-up:** an allowance representing empty space in the arch due to arch height (higher arch → more fill-up); added to instep girth. Verbatim (3DShoemaker): "the instep girth fill-up accounts for the arch height of the foot given a higher arch will leave empty space in the shoe."
- **Backseam / back-height (topline height) parameters:** back-cone top plane, backseam tack height, back height — set where the rear upper terminates (mostly relevant to boots and pattern work).

### G. Bottom / plantar geometry parameters
- **Featherline / feather-edge type:** the bottom-edge geometry — a hard ~90° corner (required for most welted construction) vs a rounded/soft edge. For Goodyear welt a sharp featherline is needed so the lasting allowance folds around it and the insole/holdfast seats on a flat bottom. *(Shoemakers Academy; USPTO 6,014,824: "pulling the lasting allowance around the last bottom featherline… secured to the flat sole surface.")*
- **Feather-edge (girth) allowance:** extra girth added because the last has sharp edges the foot lacks; applied to the girths. Verbatim (3DShoemaker): "The feather edge allowances apply only to the girths and account for the additional girth amount due to the fact that shoe lasts have sharp edges whereas feet don't."
- **Last bottom (sole) surface / bottom curve:** the plantar surface; can be flat (traditional welted) or foot-shaped (needs an arch-height parameter for foot-shaped bottoms).
- **Bottom profile / longitudinal rocker curve:** the side-profile curve of the bottom encoding toe spring + heel height + wedge angle.
- **Heel curve (rear profile):** the vertical curved back profile of the heel/seat; the sources stress this must retain its anatomical shape when length is altered.
- **Toe flare / swing (inflare–straight–outflare):** the medial/lateral swing of the forepart relative to the heel-centre axis; most modern lasts are slight inflare ("banana"). Set by the central axis vs heel axis relationship.
- **Seat / heel-seat curvature:** the plantar curvature under the heel where the foot heel docks; 3DShoemaker notes the back curvature of the template is where the back of the foot docks (which slightly reduces final bespoke length).
- **Insert / insole depth (bottom drop):** the last bottom is dropped by the insert depth (default 0.5 mm sock lining; up to ~3 mm for orthotics) to make room and also updates girths. Verbatim (3DShoemaker): "The standard insert depth for all shoe lasts is 0.5mm, which is enough space for a sock lining. But if the intended footwear will require additional space for orthotics etc., then you can specify the desired amount."

### H. Cross-sectional / quadrant parameters (the modelling backbone)
- **Girth cross-section curves:** closed section curves at the ball, waist, instep, and heel planes; their perimeters equal the target girths and their shape is tuned to hit both girth and width.
- **Wall quadrants:** each cross-section is divided into quadrants (medial-upper, lateral-upper, medial-lower, lateral-lower). 3DShoemaker lets you edit all quadrants from the various cross-section views simultaneously; quadrant control lets you bias volume medially/laterally and upper/lower independently — matching the CAD literature's "asymmetric/anisotropic girth scaling" that emphasizes one side of the metatarsal area.
- **Body wall curve:** the longitudinal curve governing the main body walls; "a smooth body wall curve is critical to achieving a smooth shoe last surface," and the body parameterization is largely reusable across last styles.
- **Toe wall curve:** the curve governing the medial/lateral toe-wall heights; raising these adds material at the toe for smoothness.
- **Cap (ridge) height:** the height of the top/cap of each cross-section curve (heel, arch, girth sections); mismatch between heel/arch caps and girth caps causes surface defects and cone distortion (the specific problem diagnosed in the smoothing article).
- **Clipping-plane position:** a modelling/inspection parameter (slider) to view any cross-section for smoothness.
- **Control-vertex grid density:** in NURBS last CAD, ~10–20 horizontal subdivisions × 30–40 vertical slices, spaced denser where the curvature radius is smaller (a defensible default for a Blender/NURBS pipeline; from the LastDesigner papers).

### I. Surface-quality parameters
- **NURBS surface decomposition:** last volume = sole surface + upper surface + top (cone) surface; the boundary between sole and upper is the feather curve, and the boundary between upper and top is the top curve (from the academic LastDesigner system — a good template for a Blender build).
- **Curvature continuity / tangency:** adjacent surfaces should meet with tangency (G1) or curvature (G2) continuity; Rhino zebra/curvature tools verify this — replicate with Blender surface/curvature checks.
- **Cap-height balancing, add/subtract material:** the parametric analog of hand sanding/lay-up; raise/lower cross-section caps and wall-curve heights to remove lumps.
- **Show-surfaces-when-updating / shoe-last transparency:** visualization toggles that trade speed for feedback while tuning.
- **Smoothness by view + clipping planes:** inspect from standard views and multiple clipped cross-sections.

### J. Sizing, grading & the foot→last relationship
- **Size & width indices:** an index system reduces the parameter count; indices map to US/UK men's/women's/children's sizes; you look up the index on the grading chart and the build form populates the measurements.
- **UK length grading:** one full size = 1/3 inch = ~8.47 mm (barleycorn); half size = 1/6 inch ≈ 4.23 mm. Per Wikipedia's *Barleycorn (unit)*: "The barleycorn is an English unit of length equal to 1⁄3 of an inch (i.e. about 8.47 mm). It is still used as the basis of shoe sizes in some English-speaking countries."
- **Ball-girth grading:** ~1/4 inch = 6.35 mm per full size; each width fitting (e.g., F→G) adds ~1/4 inch ≈ 6.35 mm (a real UK maker uses ~6 mm per fitting step). UK men's "standard" fitting is F for some makers (Spring Line, Cheaney) but E for others (Edward Green, Crockett & Jones) — flag this for the fit logic.
- **Foot→last allowances:** last length = foot length + length allowance (~17 mm round toe per 3DShoemaker; 12–15 mm everyday, 15–20 mm pointed per Koleff); girths = foot girths + feather-edge allowance + instep fill-up; plus sock thickness (nylon 0.5, dress 1, casual 1.5, sport 2, wool 3 mm) and insert depth (sock lining 0.5, cushioned 1, functional orthotic 2, accommodative 3 mm).
- **Docking nuance:** because the foot heel docks against the last's back curvature (not the bottom edge), the final bespoke last length is slightly less than the build-form length — encode as a small correction term.
- **Numeric anchor (welted men's):** Joseph Cheaney & Sons "125" last (official size guide), UK 8 F fit = 290 mm length / 245 mm joint girth; UK 9 F = 298 / 251 mm (+8 mm length, +6 mm girth per full size); UK 8 G = 251 mm joint girth (F→G = +6 mm at every size 6–12.5). 3DShoemaker's own rule of thumb: "we generally recommend not deviating by more than 5% from the corresponding measurement of a shoe last closest in size."

## Recommendations
1. **Model as a landmark-anchored, cross-section/quadrant NURBS system in Blender.** Stage 1: build a size-scaled skeleton placing heel point, ball points (medial 69%, lateral 63% of foot length), instep point (~mid-functional length), toe point, and back-cone top. Stage 2: place girth section curves (ball, waist, instep, long/short heel) on planes anchored to those landmarks; give each curve four quadrant controls. Stage 3: loft sole/upper/top NURBS with enforced tangency. Stage 4: apply toe spring + heel height + wedge as a bottom-profile transform. Stage 5: cut the feather edge (hard ~90° for welt). This mirrors both 3DShoemaker and the peer-reviewed LastDesigner architecture (sole + upper + top surfaces joined at feather curve and top curve).
2. **Expose exactly the 3DShoemaker "primary" parameters as the public API** and compute the rest: `last_length` (functional + stylistic extension), `ball_girth`, `waist_girth`, `instep_girth`, `long_heel_girth`, `ball_width`, `heel_height`, plus `toe_spring`, `feather_edge_type`, and the allowance block. Everything else (waist width, cap heights, wall curves, quadrant biases) should default from a template and be overridable — consistent with "all other measurements are calculated from these."
3. **Implement grading from UK size inputs** with length +8.47 mm/full size, ball girth +6.35 mm/full size, +6.35 mm per width fitting, and half-size +4.23 mm; seed the absolute values from a welted-maker reference (Cheaney 125: UK 8 F = 290/245 mm; UK 9 F = 298/251 mm) and validate against 3DShoemaker's chart. Default men's standard width to F but make it configurable (E for "sleek" English lasts).
4. **Encode foot→last allowances as an explicit, inspectable layer** (length/room, feather-edge girth, instep fill-up, sock, insert) so fit logic is auditable; default round-toe length allowance ~17 mm, dress-sock 1 mm, sock-lining insert 0.5 mm, and add the feather-edge girth allowance only to the girth curves.
5. **Bake in Goodyear-welt constraints:** hard ~90° featherline, a flat last bottom/seat for the insole and holdfast, and a modest feather-edge girth allowance; keep heel height in the 18–25 mm band and toe spring 13–19 mm for dress norms.
6. **Add automated surface-quality gates** before 3D-print export: curvature/zebra-equivalent continuity checks, cap-height balancing between heel/arch and girth sections, and multi-plane cross-section sampling — the digital equivalents of the smoothing article's manual checks (view-based inspection, clipping planes, transparency).
7. **Thresholds that change the plan:** if fit feedback shows heel slippage, decouple heel width/seat from ball girth (combination-last logic); if creasing/comfort complaints arise, revisit the toe-spring vs heel-height balance ("pencil under toe, penny under heel"); if any generated girth deviates >5% from the nearest reference last, re-derive allowances rather than blindly scaling (per 3DShoemaker's ±5% guidance).

## Caveats
- **Most "software" primary sources are the same product.** Sources 1, 3, 4, and 5 (and effectively 2) are all 3DShoemaker/ShoeLastMaker (Podohub is the same vendor). The parameter scheme is well-corroborated internally but reflects one vendor's parametric philosophy; the academic NURBS-last papers and traditional lastmaking manuals (Koleff, shoemaking.wiki) are used to triangulate.
- **Images could not be pixel-read by the tools.** The quadrant image (Source 4) and the parametric-software screenshot (Source 3) were interpreted via their host articles and the identical documented 3DShoemaker build form/quadrant editor rather than by OCR; named fields attributed to the screenshot (paired foot vs last measurement columns, size/width indices, allowances, template pickers) are inferred from the matching documented build form, not read letter-by-letter from the JPEG.
- **Exact per-size waist, instep, and heel-girth tables for men's UK lasts are not published openly** — they live inside 3DShoemaker's paid software or in printed manuals. Public figures exist only as relationships (waist ≈ ball − 3–5 mm; long heel = short heel + 40–46 mm) plus one welted maker's ball-girth/length table (Cheaney). Treat any absolute waist/instep/heel-girth number as **derived**, not directly sourced.
- **"Standard" width and heel figures vary by maker** (F vs E standard; 18 vs 22 vs 25 mm heel), so hardcoding a single value is risky — parametrize them.
- **Toe-spring/heel-height numbers are style-dependent** and drawn from UK last-maker product specs (Spring Line) and commentary; they are typical ranges, not standards. Similarly, the 63–69% / 2-of-3 heel-to-ball ratios are anthropometric norms, not fixed constants, and individual feet deviate.
